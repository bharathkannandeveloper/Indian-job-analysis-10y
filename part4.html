Phase 4: 2036-2040 — The AGI Question

If Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) achieves functional deployment by 2040, employment undergoes a phase change, not gradual evolution. Leading AI researchers' median estimate: P(AGI by 2040) = 25-35%. This is LOW probability but HIGH impact.

AGI Scenario: What Survives?

If AGI becomes real, approximately 60-70% of 2024 job categories don't exist in recognizable form by 2040. What survives:

  • Complex physical work in unstructured environments (plumbing in old buildings, electrical in unique industrial setups): ~75% survival
  • Healthcare requiring human touch (surgery, complex care, end-of-life): ~60%, heavily AI-assisted
  • Creative work where human experience IS the value (writing memoir, original art, performance): ~50-60%
  • Political/social leadership (humans governing humans): ~80%
  • Spiritual/religious roles: ~90%
  • High-stakes negotiation, diplomacy: ~65%
  • Childcare and elderly care (emotional work): ~70%

Non-AGI Scenario (P = 65-75%): Narrow AI Continues

More likely: AI continues automating TASKS within jobs, not entire professions. By 2040:

  • ~30-40% of work activities are AI-performed
  • Most jobs exist but are "AI-augmented" versions
  • Employment in recognizable form: ~70-80% of 2024 categories still exist
  • The displacement is real but manageable: 3.5-4.5 crore over 16 years = ~22-28 lakh/year, which formal economy CAN absorb if other sectors grow
  • The challenge: the displaced and the newly created jobs require DIFFERENT skills. The transition pain is the crisis, not the endpoint.
💬 In plain terms: The AGI question is: do you prepare for a world where machines do EVERYTHING better, or a world where machines do SOME THINGS better? The honest answer: prepare for the second, hope it's not the first. If you're a 20-year-old in 2026 choosing a career, choose something that combines: human judgment + AI tools + domain expertise + skills that take years to build. Don't choose: anything that's purely rules-based, anything a smart 18-year-old could learn in 6 months, or anything that's just "following a process." Those jobs have a half-life of 3-7 years.

💻 3.1 IT & Software Services: The Sector That Built and Hollowed the Middle Class STRUCTURAL DECLINE

The Numbers (Grounded in Reality)

FY24 revenue: $254 billion (NASSCOM). Employment: 54 lakh (5.4 million, March 2023 data). Plus ~1.4 crore in extended ecosystem (hardware, retail, services). India's IT-BPM is 7.4% of GDP. Exports: $194B (79% of revenue). This is the sector that created India's middle class in 1995-2020. By 2030-2035, it might be the sector that hollows it.

The 2024-2030 Arc: From Growth Engine to Efficiency Machine

Peak IT employment: 2022-2023 at ~57-58 lakh. FY24: net reduction of ~60,000 (TCS, Infosys, Wipro combined). FY25: further flat to negative. By 2030: NITI Aayog worst case: 60 lakh (down from 75-80L if you include IT-BPM combined in 2023). KAALCHAKRA estimate: 48-55 lakh (IT services + BPM), with GCCs at 25-30 lakh. Total IT ecosystem: ~42-48 lakh from 54 lakh in 2023.

The GCC Story: The ONLY Growth Area

India hosts 1,700+ GCCs (2025), up from ~1,580 in 2024, employing ~1.7-2 million. These are NOT traditional IT services. GCCs are: multinational R&D centers, product development hubs, AI labs, cybersecurity operations, strategic innovation centers. They hire the TOP 10-15% of Indian engineering talent. Average GCC salary: Rs 12-35 lakh (vs IT services Rs 3.5-8 lakh for same experience).

GCC growth projection: 2,100-2,500 centers by 2030, employing 25-30 lakh. This IS real job creation and high-quality. But it serves only the top-tier talent. The bottom 60-70% of engineering graduates who would have entered IT services in 2015 have NO path into GCCs in 2025. The quality bar has risen beyond their reach.

POSITIVE: GCC ecosystem represents India transitioning from cost advantage to capability advantage. Global companies are building their most strategic functions in India. Coimbatore, Jaipur, Kochi, Chandigarh (Tier-2 cities) now have GCCs. If this trend continues, India becomes a INNOVATION hub, not just outsourcing destination. This is the path to Scenario A (Leapfrog). P(GCC model becomes India's dominant tech employment story by 2035) = 55%. But this also means SMALLER total employment in tech at HIGHER productivity and wages. The mass employment IT model is dead. The elite employment GCC model is thriving. That's not good news for the average engineering graduate.

What Jobs Are ALREADY Gone (2024-2026 Evidence)

Tech hiring openings: 1.03 lakh (Jan 2026), down 60% from 2.6 lakh (early 2022). This is a 1.5+ lakh job deficit that exists right now. Where did those jobs go? They were absorbed by AI productivity. Former HCL CEO Vineet Nayar (March 2024): "Skills for coding, testing, maintenance, responding to trouble tickets will be taken over by AI. These skills will become obsolete."

Over 500,000 tech layoffs globally from 2022-April 2024 (includes India). Attrition: IT-BPM has the HIGHEST employee attrition rate in India, driven by workers seeing the writing on the wall and jumping ship before pushed.

The 2031-2040 Endgame

By 2035, India's IT sector employs 38-45 lakh (down from 54 lakh in 2023). Revenue: $300-350B+ (NASSCOM's target, likely achievable). Revenue per employee: UP 80-100%. The sector is MORE valuable but employs FEWER people. Those employed are paid MORE (top-quartile salaries rising). But the bottom 50% of the sector's 2023 workforce is gone. They're in gig economy, they've emigrated, they're in government exam prep, or they've left urban centers entirely.

By 2040, "IT job" in India means: AI/ML engineering, cloud architecture, cybersecurity, product management, or senior technical leadership. The $25,000/year coder position that employed lakhs from 2000-2025 doesn't exist anymore. The ladder that lifted millions into middle class has been retracted.

💬 In plain terms: If you're an IT employee in 2026, your sector isn't dying. It's TRANSFORMING into something that needs 60-70% as many people as it does today. The question isn't whether IT will exist in 2040. It will, and it'll be huge. The question is whether YOUR job within IT will exist, and whether your skills are in the top 40% that survives the transformation or the bottom 60% that gets restructured. If you can't honestly answer "I'm in the top 40%," you need a plan B. Not in 5 years. Now.

PLI Scheme: The Honest Accounting

Total PLI outlay: Rs 1.97 lakh crore across 14 sectors. Jobs promised: 60 lakh. Jobs created (as of mid-2025): 8-15 lakh (estimates vary; government doesn't publish consolidated employment data). Cost per job: Rs 13-25 lakh of government incentive per job created. For comparison, MGNREGS creates a job-day at ~Rs 300-350. The PLI is expensive per job but creates FORMAL, higher-quality employment.

The gap between promise and delivery exists because: (a) PLI incentivizes CAPEX (capital investment), not jobs. A Rs 1,000 Cr factory can be highly automated. (b) Many PLI beneficiaries are import-substitution, not export-creation, so volume doesn't scale as hoped. (c) Implementation lag: land acquisition, environmental clearances, skilling pipeline gaps delay projects 18-36 months.

The Automation Race: When Do Robots Cost Less Than Indians?

This is the central question. Industrial robot cost (per unit deployed): ~$35,000-50,000 in 2024. Operating cost: ~$5,000/year. Lifespan: 10-15 years. Amortized cost: ~$8,500-10,000/year per robot for a 3-shift operation.

Indian factory worker cost: Salary Rs 12,000-18,000/month = Rs 1.4-2.2 lakh/year + PF + ESI + compliance = ~Rs 2-2.8 lakh/year = ~$2,400-3,400/year. Wait. Indian labor is STILL 2.5-4x cheaper than a robot?

Yes. Which is why automation in India is DELAYED compared to China or developed countries. This is actually a temporary employment advantage. The problem: robot costs are falling 15-20% every 2-3 years. Indian wages are rising 8-12% annually. The crossover happens around 2030-2033 for assembly work, 2033-2036 for quality control, 2035-2038 for complex manufacturing. India has a 10-15 year window where human labor maintains cost advantage. After that, robots win on economics.

THE MANUFACTURING TRAP FOR A DEVELOPING COUNTRY: India must: (a) create 1-2 crore manufacturing jobs by 2032, (b) BEFORE robots become cheaper than humans, (c) while ALSO ensuring those workers are skilled enough to work alongside automation. If India moves too fast into automated manufacturing (to be globally competitive), it doesn't create enough jobs. If it moves too slow (to preserve jobs), it loses competitiveness to Vietnam/Bangladesh. Threading this needle is the most important industrial policy challenge of the 2020s. P(India successfully threads the needle) = 35-40%.
💬 In plain terms: Manufacturing was supposed to be India's answer to "what happens when IT jobs go away?" And it IS creating jobs, real ones, 4-6 lakh per year. But that's a garden hose trying to fill a swimming pool. India needs 15-25 lakh manufacturing jobs per year for a decade to truly absorb the workforce. And the clock is ticking because in 10-12 years, robots will be cheaper than humans for most factory work. India is in a race against robots, and robots don't get tired or ask for raises. If you're betting your employment future on manufacturing, make sure you're learning robot maintenance and programming, not just assembly line work.
🌾 3.3 Agriculture: The Invisible Majority Under Climate Stress CLIMATE BOMB

The Productivity Crisis in One Stat

Agriculture employs 42-45% of India's workforce but produces only ~15-16% of GDP. This 3:1 ratio (employment share to GDP share) is the single clearest indicator of disguised unemployment. For comparison: services employ ~30% and produce ~55% of GDP. That's productive employment. Agriculture is body-absorption, not value-creation, for the marginal worker.

The Climate Employment Time Bomb

By 2035, climate change's employment impact in agriculture:

  • Crop yield volatility: +25-35% compared to 2010-2020 baseline (IPCC India projections)
  • Monsoon pattern shifts: 15-20% higher chance of delayed/failed monsoon in rain-dependent regions
  • Groundwater depletion critical: Punjab, Haryana, western UP, parts of TN. ~30-40% of wells going dry
  • Heat stress: number of "too hot to work" days increasing 20-30% in agricultural belt by 2035
  • Coastal salinity ingress: affecting 3-5% of agricultural land in coastal districts

Agricultural employment vulnerable to climate by 2035: 3-5 crore workers (15-20% of agricultural workforce). These aren't job losses in the traditional sense. It's land becoming unviable, crops failing consistently, water not available. The work doesn't exist because the natural resource base doesn't support it anymore.

In the US (1900-1970), agriculture to manufacturing. In China (1990-2020), rural to urban manufacturing. In India (2024-2040): agriculture to... gig economy? Construction (which is itself automating)? The absorption pathways are far weaker than historical precedent.

Climate is making it worse. The dream that your parents' farming life continues is not realistic for 60-70% of farming families. This isn't pessimism. It's arithmetic. The question isn't whether to leave agriculture. It's WHERE you go after.

Blinkit/Zepto/Instamart 10-min grocery delivery Rs 12,000-18,000 Rs 10,000-16,000 Market consolidating. Survivors squeeze labor costs.
Freelance Platforms (Upwork, etc.) Digital freelancers Rs 20,000-50,000 Rs 15,000-40,000 Global competition + AI tools commoditizing basic freelance work.

The pattern: gig earnings DECLINE as worker supply increases. When 77 lakh becomes 2.35 crore, per-worker earnings compress. The market for deliveries and rides is not growing 3x. The worker supply is. Result: more workers sharing the same pool of earnings = everyone earns less.

Is Gig Economy Employment or Disguised Unemployment?

A delivery partner earning Rs 13,000/month, working 11 hours/day, 6.5 days/week, with zero benefits, zero pension, zero paid leave, zero health insurance, and an algorithm controlling their income is technically "self-employed" in PLFS data. Functionally, this is daily wage labor mediated by an app. The app provides the ILLUSION of formality (UPI payments, GST registration) to what is essentially piece-rate survival work.

Wealth distribution in gig economy: Platform captures 20-30% of transaction value as commission. Payment gateway, logistics, tech: another 5-10%. Worker gets: 60-75% of end-customer payment. Of that, they spend on bike fuel/maintenance (15-20%), phone and data (5%), leaving net ~50-55% as actual income. For a Rs 500 order, platform takes Rs 100-150, customer pays delivery fee Rs 30-50, worker nets Rs 25-30 for 20-30 minutes of work including wait time. That's Rs 50-90/hour. Minimum wage in most states: Rs 350-450/day (8 hours) = Rs 44-56/hour. Gig workers are earning AROUND minimum wage, not above it, with 10x the risk (vehicle maintenance, fuel, accidents, zero security).

THE SATURATION POINT: Gig economy as unemployment buffer has a ceiling. When too many workers join, per-worker earnings fall below SURVIVAL level. We're approaching this in metros. Bangalore, Mumbai, Delhi delivery earnings per order: down 15-20% from 2022 to 2024 as worker supply exploded. The buffer is saturating. By 2030, if gig worker count reaches 2.35 crore as NITI Aayog projects, average gig earnings will likely fall to Rs 10,000-14,000/month, which is BELOW the cost of dignified urban survival. At that point, gig stops being a buffer and becomes a poverty trap with an app.

The 2035-2040 Question: Autonomous Delivery

P(autonomous drones/robots displacing 30%+ of delivery workers in India metros by 2035) = 25%. India's infrastructure chaos (narrow lanes, chaotic traffic, theft risk) DELAYS this. By 2040, P rises to 40-45%. The buffer is real but finite. India's bad infrastructure is ironically job-protective for delivery workers. If roads were German-quality, delivery jobs would already be 50% automated.

💬 In plain terms: If you're a gig worker in 2026, you're not "building a career." You're buying time until you find something better. That's fine as a temporary strategy. It's catastrophic as a 10-year plan. The gig economy in 2026 is like a life raft after a shipwreck. It keeps you alive. But you can't live on a life raft forever. The problem: the rescue ship (formal employment) isn't coming for most people. So millions are just... staying on the raft, indefinitely, while the raft slowly deflates (earnings declining). This is the gig economy in India by 2030.

💬 In plain terms: If you're 18-22 years old in 2026 and asking "What field has a future?", green energy is the answer. Solar panel installation and maintenance, EV charging infrastructure, battery technology, green hydrogen plants, wind turbine technicians, energy auditing, green building certification, climate adaptation services. These jobs are growing 20-30% per year, they can't be done remotely (local presence required = less global competition), and they can't be fully automated (require human decision-making in variable environments). A solar technician earning Rs 25,000-40,000/month in 2026 will earn Rs 35,000-60,000/month in 2035, have job security, and be in a sector that's GROWING while IT is shrinking. This is the actual smart career move for the median Indian youth.

Allied health workers (physiotherapists, lab techs, optometrists, radiographers, emergency medical technicians): MASSIVE shortage. Why Healthcare is AI-Resistant (Mostly)

AI AUGMENTS healthcare rather than replacing it. AI diagnostics assist doctors, they don't replace the human who interprets, explains to patient, and makes treatment decisions considering the full context. Administrative AI reduces paperwork. Drug discovery AI accelerates pharma R&D. But patient care, surgery, rehabilitation, mental health, elderly care all require human touch. Net healthcare employment impact of AI by 2035: POSITIVE (+12-18%). AI actually CREATES healthcare jobs by: (a) making healthcare more accessible (telemedicine, vernacular AI health advisors), (b) expanding preventive care, (c) enabling more complex procedures at lower cost, increasing demand.

This is ONE OF THE FEW SECTORS where AI and employment are not in opposition. They're synergistic.

IF INDIA ACTS ON THIS: Healthcare could employ 1.5-2 crore by 2035 (from ~1.2 Cr today). These are: (a) Jobs that CANNOT be offshored (you can't do physiotherapy remotely from Vietnam), (b) AI-resistant (human touch required), (c) Growing in demand (aging + rising incomes + health awareness), (d) Accessible to women (nursing, care work socially acceptable), (e) Spread across Tier-2/3 cities, not just metros. This is THE ANSWER to "where do displaced IT/BPO workers go?" IF India invests in training infrastructure at 3-4x current scale. Cost: Rs 25,000-40,000 Cr over 5 years. Compared to PLI's Rs 1.97 lakh crore, this is CHEAP. But healthcare training doesn't create GDP growth headlines the way a semiconductor fab does, so it's politically less sexy. This is policy failure by ignorance.
💬 In plain terms: If you're looking for a career that has job security in 2040, trains relatively quickly (9 months to 2 years for most allied health roles), pays decently (Rs 20,000-50,000/month depending on role and experience), and genuinely helps people, healthcare is the answer. Not doctor (that's 5.5 years + highly competitive). But nurse, physiotherapist, lab tech, optometry assistant, dental hygienist, mental health counselor, elderly care specialist. India needs MILLIONS of these workers. The jobs exist. The training seats don't. If the government opened 200,000 nursing seats tomorrow, they'd all fill within a year. This is the opposite of the IT problem (too many graduates, not enough jobs). Healthcare is: not enough graduates, abundant jobs.
🎥 3.7 Creator Economy: The Vernacular Content Explosion MIXED OUTLOOK

The Current Landscape

Indians earning primary income from content creation (YouTube, Instagram, freelance writing/video, podcasting, streaming): 15-25 lakh (2024). Total earning ANY income part-time: ~80 lakh to 1 crore. By 2030: primary income creators could reach 40-65 lakh. By 2035: 60-90 lakh.

The Vernacular Explosion (Genuine Structural Shift)

Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, Bengali, Gujarati, Kannada, Malayalam, Punjabi content consumption growing 30-45% CAGR. English content plateauing. This creates employment across 22 scheduled languages in: video creation, editing, voice-over, writing, translation, subtitling, production, distribution. By 2030, vernacular digital content could employ 50-80 lakh across creation and production. This is REAL and happening.

The AI Threat: When Content is Free

When AI generates video, music, images, and text at near-zero cost, what happens to human creators? P(AI-generated content exceeding 50% of online content by 2030) = 50%. Human creators survive through: authenticity (lived experience), personal brand (parasocial relationships), local context (AI struggles with hyperlocal cultural nuance), and live performance (concerts, events, streams where human IS the product).

But the bottom 60-70% of creators (template content, compilation channels, generic information, basic entertainment) face existential pressure. AI can generate "10 Facts About Taj Mahal" video with voiceover and visuals in 90 seconds. A human creator takes 2 hours. The human can't compete on cost. They can only compete on QUALITY and UNIQUENESS. Most can't.

The Earnings Distribution

Top 1% of creators: earning Rs 10 lakh+/month. Top 10%: Rs 50,000-10 lakh/month. Middle 40%: Rs 15,000-50,000/month. Bottom 50%: Rs 3,000-15,000/month (supplementary income, not primary). By 2035, AI compresses the bottom 50% toward zero. The top 20-30% thrives (because they offer what AI can't). The middle 20-30% struggles (AI commoditizing what they do). Winner-take-most dynamics apply to creator economy even more than traditional employment.

THE CREATOR PARADOX: Creator economy is simultaneously: (a) A genuine new employment sector employing millions, (b) Winner-take-most where top 10% captures 70-80% of earnings, (c) Saturated with competition (millions trying, few succeeding), (d) AI-vulnerable for bottom tier but AI-enhanced for top tier. It's an opportunity AND a trap. If you have unique talent, lived experience, or charisma, it's viable. If you're "just another gaming channel" or "just another motivational speaker," you're competing with 10 lakh other channels plus AI-generated content. P(median creator earning above median formal sector wage in 2035) = 25%. For most, creator economy is a side hustle, not a career.
💬 In plain terms: The creator economy in 2026 looks like the gold rush. Everyone hears about the YouTuber making Rs 5 lakh/month and thinks "I can do that." What they don't see: 10,000 YouTubers making Rs 2,000/month. If you have a SPECIFIC talent (you're genuinely funny, you have unique expertise, you're exceptional at storytelling), creator economy is viable. If your plan is "I'll make generic content and monetize with AdSense," you'll earn less than a delivery partner by 2028. The creator economy rewards the top 10% extraordinarily and ignores the bottom 90% completely. Decide which decile you're actually in before quitting your job to become a content creator.
